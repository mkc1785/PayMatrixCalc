PSU Pay Revision 2026: Which Companies and When?

📅 April 2026 · 8 min read · Written by a Central Govt PSU employee
Home › Blog › PSU Pay Revision 2026: Which Companies and When?

The next major PSU pay revision is a hot topic, especially with the ongoing discussions around the 8th Central Pay Commission (CPC). While the exact timeline and specific companies are still being finalized, the process is expected to align with the recommendations of the 8th CPC, which is anticipated to be constituted and submit its report around 2026-2027. This means that most Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs) will see their pay scales, allowances, and benefits revised based on these new government guidelines. The Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) plays a crucial role in overseeing these revisions for all CPSEs.

Understanding the PSU Pay Revision Cycle

Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs) in India generally follow a pay revision cycle that is closely linked to the Central Government's pay commissions. The last major pay revision for PSUs was based on the 7th CPC recommendations, which came into effect for government employees from January 1, 2016, and for most PSUs shortly thereafter. The process typically involves:

  • Constitution of the Pay Commission: The Central Government establishes a Pay Commission to review and recommend changes to pay structures, allowances, and pensionary benefits for its employees.
  • Submission of Report: The Pay Commission submits its report with recommendations.
  • Government Acceptance: The Union Cabinet considers and approves the recommendations, often with modifications.
  • DPE Guidelines: The Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) then issues guidelines to all CPSEs based on the accepted recommendations.
  • Board Approval and Implementation: Individual PSUs, through their respective Boards, adopt and implement the revised pay scales and allowances. This often includes negotiations with employee unions.

Given that the 8th CPC is expected to be constituted and submit its report around 2026-2027, the actual implementation of the revised pay scales in PSUs would likely follow. This means we could see the new pay structures becoming effective from January 1, 2027, or later, depending on the speed of the process.

Which Companies are Affected by the PSU Pay Revision?

The pay revision primarily affects Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs) that are under the administrative control of various ministries and departments of the Central Government. These are companies where the government holds a significant stake. Some of the prominent sectors and examples of PSUs that will be impacted include:

  • Oil and Gas: ONGC, IOCL, BPCL, HPCL, GAIL, OIL India.
  • Banking and Financial Services: SBI (though it has its own specific pay structure, it often aligns), PNB, Bank of Baroda, Canara Bank, LIC, NABARD.
  • Manufacturing and Engineering: BHEL, SAIL, HAL, BEL, HMT, Cochin Shipyard.
  • Mining and Metals: NALCO, NMDC, CIL.
  • Power: NTPC, NHPC, Power Grid Corporation.
  • Telecommunications: BSNL, MTNL.
  • Defense Production: DRDO labs (though often treated differently), Ordnance Factory Board (now corporatized entities).
  • Other Sectors: Airport Authority of India (AAI), Food Corporation of India (FCI), Hindustan Paper Corporation, etc.

It's important to note that while the broad framework comes from the CPC recommendations and DPE guidelines, the specific implementation details, including the effective date and the exact pay scales, can vary slightly from one PSU to another. Some PSUs might have their own internal pay revision committees that work in tandem with the central guidelines.

What to Expect in the 2026 PSU Pay Revision?

The 8th CPC will likely consider several factors while formulating its recommendations. Based on past trends and current economic conditions, we can anticipate changes in:

  • Basic Pay Scales: New pay matrices will be introduced, likely leading to an increase in basic pay for all employees.
  • Dearness Allowance (DA): The DA rate is revised twice a year (January and July) based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI). As of January 2026, the DA rate for Central Government employees is expected to be around 60%. This rate will continue to be a benchmark for PSUs.
  • Allowances: Various allowances such as House Rent Allowance (HRA), Transport Allowance, Children Education Allowance, etc., are usually revised. The HRA slabs, for instance, are typically linked to the DA rate and city classification (X, Y, Z). With DA expected to be high, HRA rates might also see adjustments.
  • Promotions and Career Progression: The pay commission might suggest changes to promotion policies and career progression paths.
  • Pensionary Benefits: For employees who joined before the National Pension System (NPS) or for those covered under the old pension scheme, pensionary benefits will also be reviewed.

The Role of Dearness Allowance (DA)

Dearness Allowance is a crucial component of salary for government and PSU employees. It's designed to compensate for the rising cost of living. The DA rate is calculated as a percentage of the basic pay and is revised periodically based on the average All India Consumer Price Index (AICPI). As of January 2026, the DA rate for Central Government employees is projected to be around 60%. This means that for every 100 rupees of basic pay, employees receive an additional 60 rupees as DA. This rate is expected to continue its upward trend, influencing the overall salary structure.

DA Rate as of January 2026: Approximately 60%

The DA rate for PSUs is generally in sync with the Central Government's DA rates. This means that when the government announces a DA hike, PSUs typically follow suit, albeit with a slight lag sometimes.

HRA Slabs and Revisions

House Rent Allowance (HRA) is another significant component of salary. The rates for HRA are determined by the DA rate and the classification of the city or area where the employee resides. The typical slabs are:

  • X Category Cities: 30% of Basic Pay (when DA is 25% or more)
  • Y Category Cities: 20% of Basic Pay (when DA is 25% or more)
  • Z Category Cities: 10% of Basic Pay (when DA is 25% or more)

Since the DA rate is expected to be 60% by January 2026, the HRA rates would have already moved to these higher percentages. For example, an employee in an X category city with a basic pay of ₹50,000 would receive an HRA of ₹15,000 (30% of ₹50,000).

National Pension System (NPS) Implications

For employees who joined service after January 1, 2004, the National Pension System (NPS) is the primary retirement benefit. The contribution to NPS is typically 10% of basic pay plus DA, contributed by both the employee and the employer. With the increase in basic pay and DA due to the pay revision, the absolute amount contributed to NPS will also increase. This means higher contributions towards retirement savings, which is beneficial in the long run.

NPS Contribution (Employee + Employer): 20% of (Basic Pay + DA)

Projected Salary Components (Post-Revision, Hypothetical):

  • New Basic Pay (Level 7): ₹75,000
  • New DA (e.g., 60% of New Basic): ₹45,000
  • New HRA (e.g., 30% of New Basic): ₹22,500
  • Revised Other Allowances: Varies

Projected Gross Salary: ₹1,42,500 + Revised Allowances

This example illustrates a potential increase in gross salary due to the pay revision, driven by higher basic pay and consequently higher DA and HRA.

When Can We Expect the Official Announcement?

The 8th CPC is expected to be set up in late 2024 or early 2025. Its report could be submitted by mid-2026, with the government's decision and subsequent DPE guidelines potentially issued by late 2026 or early 2027. For PSUs, the implementation would then follow, aiming for an effective date of January 1, 2027, or possibly January 1, 2026, if the government decides to make it retrospective from that date, as was the case with the 7th CPC.

It's crucial for PSU employees to stay updated through official circulars from the DPE and their respective organizations. Employee unions also play a vital role in advocating for fair pay revisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the next PSU pay revision expected?

The next PSU pay revision is generally expected to be implemented based on the recommendations of the 8th Central Pay Commission, which is anticipated to be constituted and submit its report around 2026-2027. However, individual PSUs may have their own revision cycles based on their charters and government directives.

Which PSUs are covered under the pay revision?

All Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs) that are governed by the Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) guidelines are typically covered under the pay revision process. This includes a wide range of companies across various sectors.

What is the typical structure of a PSU pay revision?

PSU pay revisions usually involve a committee or board that reviews existing pay scales, allowances, and benefits. They consider factors like inflation, industry benchmarks, and government policies to propose new pay structures, often aligned with Central Government pay commissions.

How does DA (Dearness Allowance) work in PSUs?

Dearness Allowance in PSUs is revised periodically, usually twice a year (January and July), based on the average Consumer Price Index (CPI). The rate is typically linked to the DA rates announced for Central Government employees.

Will the 8th CPC recommendations impact PSU pay scales?

Yes, the recommendations of the 8th Central Pay Commission are expected to form the basis for the next major pay revision for PSUs, similar to how the 7th CPC impacted them. This will likely lead to revised pay scales, allowances, and benefits.
